Moki Rises Early

Moki lived in the trees on the edge of the forest in the Tennessee Valley where limestone caves dotted the hillsides. From the age of three, when his tribe returned from their copper pilgramge, they adorned him with feathers. Oh how he wished someday he would join them in their run. But his oddly jointed knees would always lead the tribe to leave him behind.

When not in the safety of the trees, Moki would asend to explore the caves where the forest floor met the limestone hills. Here, in the narrow crevasses, he was most adept, accustomed to pulling himself along, the strength of his arms grew and allowed him to grip with one hand while holding his light between his teeth and his drawing tool in the other.

Over the years he would create magnificent murals far beneath the earth, following the flow of underwater rivers to hugh cavernous spaces. He loved illustrating the stories of the land to the north as it was described to him by the other members of the running tribe, where he so longed to travel. He imagined himself as a bird in flight, no longer limitated by his knees.

While he was young, Moki was cared for by Shinwa, an older woman from the Jaguar Tribe who brought him food and the tools he would use to paint pots with her on the shores of the river as he developed his art of soaring heights and endless rivers.

Coda's Tribe

To understand Coda, you had to understand what made her tribe unique. They were living here in the valley separated from the larger tribes. Their secluded farms preserved a tranquil peace. There were no big migrations through the valley, only birds and butterflies came with the seasons. Their cave art was distinctly different from the other tribes. As a cave artist, Moki extended this tradition, adding a twist of his own.
