Finibus Tour

To understand the roots of this story, you'd have to track down my classic-guitar-playing roommate, Toby Shepard. Toby used to rotate his guitar sideways and play it like a lute. Then he'd jam, oh man how he'd jam, playing the most relaxing ancient Spanish tunes way into the late hours of the night.

Poor, talented Toby had a huge goose bump on the top of his head, I mean it looked like he'd been hit by the biggest cartoon baseball bat, yet he did nothing about it, except grow his hair long and nightly down a quart of whiskey while he played the most beautiful medieval guitar chords you'd ever heard.

He'd sneak into the antiquities sections of the UCSB library to get the real old classical music books. These things were lined with gold! Seriously! Most of them were created by monks, some over 1,000 years ago. And Toby would play the notes on his guitar, so sweet to the hears, tunes that hadn't been heard by human ears since the middle ages.

I guess that's how the tune got stuck in my head. Because when we laid it down as a background track for the first Finibus tour, I had no idea where it came from. Toby pointed it out to me later. Verse x10o3. He'd been converting an old latin epic to structured chords. We called the song Epicurean Delights, and it had, shall we say, a certain addictive quality. It made the girls go wild.

It always surprised me that a little tune, locked away for so many years could have become such a big hit. I mean, here's a series of notes that were as old as dirt, and they're topping the charts across the country.

Somehow I always felt we'd be a one hit wonder, so we lived things large, real large, right from the start. The reality of our success dawned on us about the time we rolled into Bakersfield.

The band had a spirit for adventure, since our flavor of dance hall reggae in Bakersfield was sort of like 2 Live Crew playing at Tipper Gore's bridge club. Finabus doesn't do any Buck Owens or Dwight Yoakam covers.

Regardlesss, the crowd was packed solid in that little road house bar, drenched in sweat as they jammed and pushed close to our stage and the deep base of our rock-steady base speaker. We were a scene unto ourselves, a single crazy pulsing light in a wide desert of dry crops and quizzical cattle.

And this was an extra special night because my old friends Derek and Liam were there to join us on stage. Derek had moved back to the east coast and joined an ashram, and Liam, master of ska, was coming through Bakersfield with his band Easy Big Fella on their way to gig in LA. This was a performance we'd never forget, the stage diving, the love of the crowd, the star studied leather jackets and spiky stars flying over our heads on the twirling girls' cowboy boots. Good times in the heart of the dusty American dream.

That night I dreamt I saw the clearest night sky you can imagine, near infinite detail. There's no way I could draw any of those star positions if you asked me now. I could maybe guess at the Milky Way and Orion, but in my sleepy mind I could somehow chart them all, as if some ancient memory were generating a crystal clear map from all the years my distant ancestors had made their way home under the same night sky.

When the front wheel chassis broke on a cattle gate in the road, we ended up trading the van to a farmer for his beat-up trailer and continued the rest of the tour in Toby's cousin's 1968 limo, our equipment lashed precariously on the trailer behind. Things were at a fever peak when we rolled into San Francisco that hot July night. That's when I met Tess, at a Berkley concert, except she wasn't there to see the show. She was working on a large tapestry that hung near the entrance to the theater. I called her over to see if she could watch the trailer while we carried in the main woofer.

When I came back, Tess was lost in thought, sitting on the edge of the bumper, legs crossed and her finger stitching a pattern across the side of her leg. I was transfixed. Her skin was a pale white, almost pearlescent and she had the coolest freckles across her shoulders and down her back.

Tess was a textile major at the time, doing some of the first digital fabric design using computers to create patterns that blew your mind. Tess was pushing the limits, merging 3D images using a new camera being developed by a friend in the science lab. The result was a dense, ever changing tapestry of fractal shapes and designs. Some days I'd just stare into the threads, lost in a maze that was crazy up-close, but even more jaw dropping from across the room. Each swirl combined with others to form whole universes of color that changed as we walked from side-to-side.

Needless to say, I was floored when she made the band's cloaks out of her finest roll of the new material. We felt like royalty, brothas from another planet.

I must admit - The common thread of my happiest memories was the glow and high after a really good show. The feeling that we really were a great band, doing the right thing. So many late nights, laughing at and reveling in the silliness of this great adventure.

From coast to coast, station to station, we traveled the world, avoiding airplanes whenever possible. The nights we spent adrift at sea were among my favorite. Here now, off the coast of Tripelee, the band snoozed on our way to Italy as waves lulled us to sleep.

Tess held me all night, the movement of the boat rocked us to sleep. Such an odd chant, hypnotic like a James Joyce novel. Tess's Latin was way better than mine, so good that she could recite it in her sleep. Weird.
